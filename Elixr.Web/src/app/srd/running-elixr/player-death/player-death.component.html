Player Death

In order for there to be high reward, there must be high risk. Adventuring is a dangerous gig, and dying is a risk that all characters carry.

Resurrection

Magic can bring a creature back to life, but it's not always an option. The longer a creature has been dead, the harder it is to cast Resurrection on that creature.

Or, maybe no creatures within the vicinity of the recently deceased that know the spell Resurrection, and dragging the corpse to someone who does is not feasible.

A New Character

If a character dies, the player may choose to create a brand new character to replace them. Allow the new character to be the same level as the recently deceased. You'll need to come up with a way to introduce them to the remaining party members, and have the party members accept the new character joining their party.

A Heroic Death

When a character dies, it can ease the blow of dying if the player feels like their character's death had a meaning in the story. Perhaps, because of their death, the other party members were able to escape the dungeon alive.

Consider other poetic ways in which a character may meet their end. This is not always easy to do on the fly, as it's rare that a character death is expected, but the effort can make for a satisfying end to a character's arc.

Other Ways of Raising the Stakes

Instead of making every encounter a life or death situation for the player's character, consider other ways to make an encounter feel urgent.

The "Time Bomb"

A concept in writing that is used to create a sense of urgengy is sometimes referred to as the "Time Bomb". It follows a simple formula: If x isn't accomplished by y, then z will occur, where z is perceived as a negative outcome.

Consider this time bomb: If the character's do not rescue the magistrate from the orcs by sundown, the magistrate will be beheaded. Here, x is "Rescuing the magistrate", y is "Sundown", and z is "magistrate is beheaded".

The characters may fail to defuse the bomb in time, in which case they are made aware that their failure was responsible for the outcome.

Reduction in power

This should be used with caution, as player's can feel like they've put hard work into their characters, only for you to invalidate that work.

In this situation, the punishment for failure isn't death, but a reduction in the character's abilities. Perhaps causing the character to lose a level, or locking a certain Feature from their use.

This reduction should always be temporary, and the parameters for regaining the power made very clear to the players. It can be made into a fun an opportunity for a "mini-quest", to restore the character's former power.
