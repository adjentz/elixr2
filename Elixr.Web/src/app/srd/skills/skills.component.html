Skills

Skills indicate how adept or well trained your character is at performing a certain action. Each Skill is detailed below.

Skills have four parts: Ranks, Modifier, Misc., and Bonus.

Ranks

You determine how versatile you want your character to be at a Skill by placing points in the ranks column.

When you first create your character, each rank starts at 0. You then have a pool of 8 Points to distribute between all of the Skills. To increase a rank by one, you reduce the pool by one, until the remaining Skill points is zero.

Ranks for a Skill can go no higher than your character's level + 3

Modifier

The Skill modifier is carried down from the relevant ability modifier. If your Strength Modifier is 3, for example, all of the Skills in the Strength section have a modifier of 3. The same follows for the other abilities.

Misc.

Various Features and magical items can give a Misc. bonus to specific Skills. This is added to the Misc. bonus of the relevent Ability.

Bonus

The sum of Ranks, Modifier, and Misc..

Making a Skill check

Your GM may call for a Skill check to be rolled when you attempt an action related to that Skill.

To roll a Skill check, take the result of 1d20 + Skill Bonus. Your GM will tell you if the result is high enough to perform the action you were attempting.

Regardless of if the check succeeds or not, the Speed cost, if any, is applied fully.

Criticaling

When rolling 1d20, if the result of the roll is 20 before any other modifiers are applied, this is referred to as a critical.

A critical indicates an automatic success, as long as the GM rules that the action isn't physically impossible, and that you have the necessary equipment, if any.

A critical also means that you performed the action so effectively, that you can add a flourish to the action for flavor, if desired.

Fumbling

When rolling 1d20, if the result of the roll is 1 before any other modifiers are applied, this is referred to as a fumble.

A fumble indicates an automatic failure, but only if a creature is under pressure, such as a combat situation. Otherwise, the roll is added normally.

On a fumble, you can still continue your turn, even to retry the check, as long as you have enough Speed remaining to do so.

Opposed Skill checks

Usually, the GM is the one that determines what the Difficulty of making a specific check is. However, that is not always the case.

Suppose a creature attempts to hide using a Stealth Check. An opponent that might want to notice the hidden creature can, on their turn, make a Perception check. If the Perception Check is greater than the result of the Stealth Check, the opponent notices the hidden creature. It is said that Stealth is Opposed by Perception.

When a skill specifies that it is opposed by another skill, record the result of the skill check. On their turn, opponents may attempt to make the specified "opposed-by" skill check. If the result of the opposed-by check is greater than the result of the original skill check, the opponent's check is then considered successful.

Skill Defenses

Some skills indicate that they have an associated "Defense". A Skill Defense is equal to 10, plus the bonus of the associated Skill.

The primary skills with Defenses are: Athletics, Acrobatics, and Concentration.

These skill Defenses are used passively to determine the Difficulty of certain actions. You can see how they might be used in the combat section of Playing Elixr.

Character Backgrounds

The GM is free to challenge what your character is capable of doing based on your character's background, or alter the Difficulty to your character.
For example, a character might want to use Engineer to craft a sword. The GM might reply "Were you a blacksmith before joining the party? Were you an apprentice to a blacksmith? Have you ever seen a blacksmith? Then no, you cannot craft a sword."

Take the opportunity to flesh out in writing what your character has done in their lifetime to have gained the skills that they have now.
